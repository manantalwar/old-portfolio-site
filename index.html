ABOUT ME

I am a graduate student in Computer Science at the Manning College of Information and Computer Sciences at the University of Massachusetts Amherst. A proficient computer programmer, data scientist, and visionary, I am driven by my passion for the field of computation and the countless possibilities that lie ahead for leveraging my skills to create a more equitable world.

My primary interest lies at the dynamic intersection of social and computer sciences. I am fascinated by the study of social and behavioral relationships and their interactions with technology through scalable computational techniques and their consequences in the real world. I am also interested in the applications of discrete and applied mathematics to computer and information sciences.

My research focuses on the application of machine learning to solve real-world problems. My most recent project focused on conceptualizing a tool for assisting parents in protecting their children from technology-driven exploitation by developing a pipeline to aggregate and classify potentially harmful app reviews. My other research projects include developing an indicator for Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) and an analysis of state-of-the-art machine learning tools for social network analysis. I am also interested in computer science education. My recent research in this domain has focused on the applications of the Process Oriented Guided Inquiry Learning (POGIL) framework to Computer Science I classrooms. I have also taken up several teaching roles at UMass Amherst, teaching both programming as well as theoretical computer science.

On the professional front, I am a self-confident, dedicated, and diplomatic individual. I have extensive experience not only in building and managing teams but also in taking them to great heights. I am a creative individual who likes to think outside of the box. I possess exceptional problem-solving and analytical abilities. I am a visionary and an impactful orator with a goal-oriented mindset.

AppDanger

June 2022 - December 2022

Skills utilized: Python, SpaCy, PostgreSQL, AGILE, AWS

Description: This python package provides support for preprocessing, training and serializing NLP text classification models on app reviews data from the Apple and Google Play stores using RoBERTa. With our partner, the UMass Rescue Lab, we conceptualized a tool for assisting parents in protecting their children from technology-driven exploitation. The idea is that parents are presented with reviews and visualizations that effectively communicate which apps pose a danger to children.

Architecture: The package comprises two efficient scrapers to aggregate app reviews from Google Play Store and Apple App Store, a SpaCy classification pipeline based on the RoBERTa transformer neural network model to run on AWS and a PostgreSQL database to store app reviews as well as model characteristics.

Important: The source code for this project is not available to the public yet. This is owing to the sensitive and triggering nature of the data as well as the fact that this project is the Center for Data Science & UMass Rescue Lab's original research. Please contact for details.

Student Learning & Motivation in Active Learning Environments in Undergraduate CS Classrooms

January 2022 - May 2022

Description: The objective of this study is to understand why and how such pedagogical approaches to Computer Science education enhance student learning and motivation and how these parameters are measured in undergraduate Computer Science classrooms. In this study, we present a systematic literature review of such pedagogical approaches and assess their impact on student learning and motivation. Subsequently, we propose an implementation for a learning model that integrates CSCL, an established learning paradigm in Computer Science education with POGIL, an evolving dynamic learning pedagogy and report the impacts of such an implementation on student learning and motivation.

Achievements: This original research project won the second prize at the Undergraduate Research Competition at the Twenty-Third Annual CCSE.
